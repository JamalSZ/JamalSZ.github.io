About Me

I recently completed my PhD in Computer Science at the University of Zurich, defending my thesis with Great Distinction. My research focused on time series predictability and information theory, combining deep learning with algorithmic modeling to advance forecasting and predictive analytics.

My academic journey began with a Bachelor's degree in Computer Engineering from Politecnico di Torino, followed by a Master's degree in Computer Science from the Free University of Bolzano, where I graduated with top honors (110/110, top 1%).

Professionally, I've worked as a Data Analyst at Durst Phototechnik AG, developing RNN and Bayesian ML models for anomaly detection. I collaborated with engineering teams to integrate predictive models into maintenance pipelines, gaining practical experience in deploying machine learning solutions.

My expertise includes Python, PyTorch, TensorFlow, and various ML techniques including transformers and neural forecasting. I've also served as a Teaching Assistant for Database Systems at the University of Zurich and have supervised several MSc and BSc theses.

Research Interests

Multivariate Time Series Predictability

Extending PhD work on univariate time series predictability to multivariate domains, developing frameworks to quantify predictability bounds for complex temporal data.

Explainable AI for Time Series

Creating interpretability methods specifically designed for time series forecasting models, making complex temporal patterns understandable to domain experts.

AutoML Integration

Integrating predictability estimation into open-source AutoML systems to enhance automated time series analysis pipelines.

My research bridges theoretical predictability bounds and practical forecasting applications. I'm expanding my PhD work to multivariate time series, which presents unique challenges due to cross-dimensional dependencies.

I'm particularly interested in how predictability estimation can enhance explainable AI for temporal data. By understanding the fundamental limits of prediction, we can better evaluate model performance and explain why certain patterns are difficult to forecast.

Another focus is integrating these concepts into open-source AutoML systems to help data scientists automatically assess forecast difficulty and select appropriate models.
